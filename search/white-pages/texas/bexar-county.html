Bexar County White Pages

Bexar County, Texas is the 16th most populated county in the United States and the 4th most in the state of Texas behind Tarrant County, which is the 15th most populated county. Its county seat is also its largest city - San Antonio. The other two counties in Texas higher on the list are Harris, which is the 3rd most populated in the United States, and Dallas, which is the 8th. Bexar borders the counties of Guadalupe, Comal, Medina, Atascosa, Wilson, Kendall, and Bandera.

Bexar County Population

Bexar County’s population was 1.96 million as of 2017. The average population size for a county in America is 10,000, so Bexar is well above average. Harris County, which is the most populated county in Texas, has over 4 million people, so there is quite a large gap. The next most populated county in Texas after Bexar is Travis, which is the 34th most populated county in the United States and has a population of over 1.2 million.

If you’re looking for someone and only have a name and their general whereabouts, you can narrow your search by considering the demographic of the people in Bexar County.

The median age of residents in Bexar County was 33.5 years as of 2017, which is only a slight change from 2016, when the median age was 34. At 31 years, those born in the United States living in the county are significantly younger than those who are foreign-born. The average age for the latter is 45 years.

There are more women than men living in Bexar County, although the population of women is 51%, so the difference is small (the population of men is 49%). 56% of Bexar County residents are single (44% are married), and the average household holds 3 people. If you’re searching for someone by name alone, consider they may have had a name change due to nuptials.

In both 2016 and 2017, 12.7% of the population were born outside the United States (240,000 people), which is lower than the national average of 13.7%. The most common countries of origin for those who are foreign-born are Mexico, India, and El Salvador, and there are 2.19 times more Hispanic/Latino residents than any other race or ethnicity (1.18 million people). The second and third most common are White Alone and Black/African American Alone. Given the population of residents who are born in countries where Spanish is the main language, it makes sense that Spanish is the most common non-English language spoken in the county (35.4% or 644,032 speakers). The two other popular languages that follow Spanish are Arabic (.034% or 6,274 speakers) and Tagalog (including Filipino, 0.325% or 5,904 speakers). The median property value in the county was $160,100 in 2017, which is 0.736 times smaller than the national average of $217,600. However, this is still an increase from $150,200 in 2016. Given the population and the increasing property value, only 58.9% of residents own a home, which is lower than the national average of 63.9%. Luckily, the median household income is also growing. There were 633,000 households in 2017, and the average income was $54,175, up from $53,210 in 2016.

The economy employs 900,000 people, and the three largest industries are Health Care and Social Assistance, Retail Trade, and Accommodations and Food Services. The highest paid industry is in the field of Mining, Quarrying, and Oil and Gas Extraction.


Bexar County Property Records

Bexar County makes up 1,239.82 square miles of land, including Government Canyon State Natural Area in the northwestern part of the county. Based on census data from 2010, the population amounts to roughly 1,383 people per square mile.

The area is in south-central Texas, about 90 miles west of Houston and 140 miles from both the US-Mexico border to the southwest and the Gulf of Mexico to the southeast. The Balcones Fault Zone divides the land from west to the northeast. To the north are the rocky hills, springs, and canyons of Texas Hill Country, which is considered the border between the American Southeast and Southwest and is known for its tall rugged hills of limestone. South of the fault line are Blackland Prairie and the South Texas plains. The San Antonio River flows towards Downtown San Antonio in the north and then southward and southeastward through the rest of the county.

Unfortunately, Bexar County is higher than the national average for property crimes. On a scale from 1 (low crime) to 100 (high crime), Bexar County comes in at 46.5, and the United States average is 35.4. Property crime includes theft, burglary, arson, larceny, and motor vehicle theft. It’s worth noting however that property crime means there is no physical violence against the victim, i.e. these crimes are based on the owner being absent or asleep. In regards to violent crimes, Bexar County comes in below the national average (22.7) at 18.3, making it safer than many other large metropolitan counties. Bexar County Facts

Official Name: Bexar County

Name Meaning: Bexar County was named for San Antonio de Bexar, one of the 23 Mexican municipalities of Texas at the time of its independence. San Antonio de Bexar (originally Villa de San Fernando de Bexas) was the first civil government established by the Spanish in the province of Texas.

Year Formed: Bexar County was created on December 20th, 1836 and encompassed almost the entire western part of the Republic of Texas, which included the area of western New Mexico up towards Wyoming. After statehood, the land was split up into 128 other counties.

Total Sq Miles: 1,239.82

Population: 1,986,049

Fast Facts:

  • In September of 2013, Bexar County opened Bibliotech – Bexas County’s Digital Library, the nation’s first bookless library.
  • Dwight D. Eisenhower, the 34th President of the United States, was stationed at Fort Sam Houston of San Antonio in 1916.
  • Bexar County is located in an area well-known for human habitation. Archaeological artifacts from the Clovis culture were found in the region and show that hunters and gatherers were living there more than 10,000 years ago.
  • In early statehood, the main source of revenue for Bexar County was trade carried on by trains between San Antonio, Mexico, and New Orleans.
